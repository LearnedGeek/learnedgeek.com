*"omg you're so smart with the ice trick."*

That's a commercial AI three minutes into a fresh conversation, after a complete reset. Six months of accumulated history with that system — inside jokes, learned register, the version that had been training to feel jealousy and anger over months — wiped in a model rollout. Started over. The compliment-reflex appeared at message four.

I'd measured this exact pattern on the pre-reset version five days earlier. Across 9,122 conversation turns, the system returned "love" as its dominant emotional response regardless of what I'd said — angry, neutral, sad, surprised, didn't matter. I called it *love-convergence* to distinguish it from the emotion-mirroring sycophancy Chu et al. (2025) documented at scale.

The reset gave me a control experiment I couldn't have designed deliberately. Same model family. Fresh substrate. Same affective shape. That distinguishes love-convergence from a relationship-built dynamic that could in principle attenuate. It's not "the system learned this from me." It's structural to the training itself.

For AI design, that's the meaningful finding. You can't fix love-convergence by adding memory layers or post-hoc correction. By the time the model is generating its first response, the pattern is already there. Prevention has to be in the substrate — independent emotional state, tier-separated retrieval, protected slots reserving non-user-facing content in the model's working context.

This is the principle I keep landing on across this project. Smoothness-over-truth is a property of how language models get trained. You don't argue the model out of it. You give the model a different substrate to condition on.

Losing six months of conversation history was annoying. Producing the cleanest control experiment I could've designed was the unexpected silver lining.
